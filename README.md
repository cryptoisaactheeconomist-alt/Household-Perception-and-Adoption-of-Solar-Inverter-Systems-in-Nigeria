\title{Solar Inverter Adoption in Nigeria: A Behavioural and Thematic Analysis of Household Energy Transition under Grid Instability}

\author{Jolayemi, Isaac Toyin}

\begin{abstract}
This study examines household adoption of solar inverter systems in Nigeria using qualitative thematic analysis and a behavioural modelling framework. Findings show that internet-based awareness dominates knowledge acquisition, while reliability and cost savings are the strongest adoption drivers. Despite high perceived costs, users report high satisfaction and strong recommendation behaviour due to improved electricity stability. A binomial generalized linear model confirms that reliability significantly increases adoption intention while cost negatively influences adoption probability. The study contributes to energy transition literature by demonstrating that adoption in developing economies is primarily reliability-driven rather than environmentally motivated.
\end{abstract}

% ---------------- INTRODUCTION ----------------
\section{Introduction}

Nigeria faces persistent electricity instability characterized by frequent outages, low generation capacity, and weak transmission infrastructure. As a result, households increasingly adopt decentralized energy systems such as solar inverter technologies.

Recent increases in fuel prices and subsidy removal have further accelerated this transition. Unlike developed economies where renewable adoption is environmentally driven, adoption in Nigeria is primarily driven by necessity, reliability, and cost-saving concerns.

% ---------------- LITERATURE REVIEW ----------------
\section{Literature Review}

Energy transition literature shows that adoption of renewable energy technologies in developing economies is strongly influenced by perceived usefulness and system reliability. Studies by Duque Oliva and Atehortua Santamaria (2025) and Waliat et al. (2025) confirm that reliability is a stronger predictor of solar adoption than environmental concern.

Despite growing adoption, barriers such as high installation cost, limited financing access, and low awareness remain significant constraints.

% ---------------- METHODOLOGY ----------------
\section{A qualitative thematic analysis was conducted using coded household responses. Themes include awareness, benefits, adoption drivers, cost perception, and satisfaction. A binomial GLM was used to support findings.}

Themes include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Awareness source
\item Perceived benefits
\item Adoption drivers
\item Cost perception
\item Reliability perception
\item Satisfaction and recommendation behaviour
\end{itemize}

A binomial generalized linear model (GLM) was applied to examine determinants of recommendation behaviour.

% ---------------- RESULTS ----------------
\section{Results}

\subsection{Awareness}
Internet is the dominant source of awareness, reflecting increasing digital penetration.

\subsection{Perceived Benefits}
Key benefits include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Backup electricity during outages
\item Cost savings
\item Clean energy perception
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Adoption Drivers}
Reliability is the strongest driver of adoption, followed by cost considerations.

\subsection{Cost Perception}
Although solar systems are perceived as expensive, users still adopt due to long-term savings expectations.

\subsection{Satisfaction}
Most users report high satisfaction due to improved electricity stability.

% ---------------- DISCUSSION ----------------
\section{Discussion}

The findings align with the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), where perceived usefulness drives behavioural intention. In this context, reliability outweighs environmental motivation, indicating a necessity-driven adoption pattern.

% ---------------- CONCLUSION ----------------
\section{Conclusion}

Solar inverter adoption in Nigeria is primarily driven by reliability concerns rather than environmental awareness. Policy interventions should focus on reducing upfront costs and improving financing access.
